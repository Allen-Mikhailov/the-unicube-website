The Start

The initial phases of our project

Our Passion

All three members of our group have been fortunate enough to go through the engineering pathway at Elkins High School, which has introduced and exposed us to a variety of engineering practices. The main two that our group was interested in are electronics and coding. We wanted to create a product that would introduce younger students in primary school to these engineering ideas, and get them interested in the field. Additionally, we believed that we could create a solution to manufacturing woes, such as cost and efficiency.

Our Problem

Implementing new robotics ideas is often very expensive, long, and arduous. Just creating slight modifications to a design will require very specialized and highly trained workers. Different ideas in robotics require a completely differently arranged workspace, even though the ideas might be similar.

Our Reason

Ever since the robot boom, there has always been an issue of robots being difficult to repurpose and communicate with. Because more efficient robotic models get created so fast, today's new models are tomorrow's old ones, becoming e-waste. Although thai happens with all forms of technology, it’s especially fast with robotics, because of the nature of how they are built and purposed; devices like computer chips and phones are more easily recyclable, and become obsolete much slower. According to Waste Management World, e-waste is the fastest growing form of waste, and that is in no small part due to robots in our society. If we are able to reduce the rate of robots becoming completely obsolete, by creating a more modular, upgradeable system, then we can halt the growth of e-waste due to robots in our society.

Our Solution

Finally, after months of anticipation and perfecting, we have created the UniCluster V1, a robust system of robotic shells that can house multiple electronics (made with the finest ABS plastic). The current iteration is shown in testing with an Arduino Uno microcontroller, 4 motors, and 1 motor controller. The system is powered with one external power bank. The total cost nears $30- far cheaper than the current solutions in the market with similar power. However, the current interaction can be used in far more configurations, such as including fans, infrared sensors, and anything that can be used in a circuit (as long as the user knows how to create them in a circuit, though).

Testing

We underwent careful and rigorous testing to ensure that we had the best solution.

Testing

We used a series of tests that we believed would test all the attributes of the UniCube that mattered: strength, time to assemble, and ease of use. For our strength test, we connected 6 more cubes to a single UniCube, and made sure it could hold up the weight of all 6 without buckling or breaking. The UniCube passed this test with ease. Next, we had randomly selected peers try and assemble the cube, given the parts and a screwdriver. All participants were able to pass this test, and stated that they found the cube creatively designed and easy to assemble. Finally, for our time to assemble the test, we timed each participant and ourselves to build the cube. Each participant was able to make the cube in under 5 minutes, passing our goal of 10 minutes on average. As such, the UniCube passed all three tests.

Conclusion

Conclusion Questions

  • Question 1

    What advantages does the web-based exchange of information have over bound printed materials?

    Bound printed materials have limitations in who can access them. When all your information is put on a piece of paper, you can only access it if you are in the same place as that piece of paper. With a website, this changes, because all you need is access to the internet, which most people have in our modern world.

  • Question 2

    What disadvantages does the web-based exchange of information have over bound printed materials?

    The new problem with a website is that it is now limited by the paper. The resources and skill sets required to make changes and convey information are much more complex compared to using bound materials. This makes it harder for the average person to convey information through a website, while anyone is able to create information in a simple physical format.

  • Question 3

    Provides a short explanation detailing the outcome of your design project.

    Our design project taught us that creating modularity is a lot more complex and difficult than what meets the eye: especially when creating an elegant solution. However, we prevailed in the end, creating a system of robotic cases that can house electronics with less limitations compared to other competitors.

  • Question 4

    Explains your successes and failures as they apply to the outcome of your Design.

    Perhaps our biggest success was that we actually created a robust, working system of cases: they can fit most electronics you would need at that size, and can easily be scaled up. However, the actual system has a lot of shortcomings. The material is ABS, which although very reliable, isn’t workable enough with robots, and gets very fragile when we need smaller details. If we want to add the extra details, we need a different material. Finally, the Unicube is also a little clunky in how it is used. Again, it fulfills its purpose, but it could be a simpler interface to use.
